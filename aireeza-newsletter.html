Alt Business Performance Framework
Find out where
your business is
quietly breaking.
15 minutes. 15 questions. A personalised score across the three pillars that determine whether your business is financially healthy, structurally sound, and founder-proof. No sign-up to start.
Time ~15 minutes
Questions 15 across 3 pillars
Output Scored report + findings
Framework Alt Business Model
Cost Free
Assessment structure — 3 pillars, 5 questions each
Pillar 01
Profit Architecture
How your business generates and retains money. Margins, pricing, revenue concentration, and whether profit is by design or by accident.
Pillar 02
Team & Cost Structure
Whether your team and cost base are built to support growth or quietly eating it. The relationship between your headcount, roles, and actual output.
Pillar 03
Systems & Founder Dependency
How much of your business lives in your head. Whether processes exist, whether they're followed, and whether you could step away without everything stopping.
Question 3 of 15
Pillar 1 of 3  —  Profit Architecture
Pillar 01
Profit Architecture
These questions look at how your business is designed to make money. Not whether you have revenue, but whether the way you generate and keep it is intentional or inherited by accident.
Question 01 / 15
When you look at your gross margin, do you know exactly what's driving it and what's eating it?
Yes. I know my margins by product, service line, or client segment and I review them regularly.
4 pts
Roughly. I have a general sense but I don't break it down at that level.
2 pts
Not really. I look at overall profit and loss but margin by segment isn't something I track.
1 pt
I'm not sure what my gross margin actually is right now.
0 pts
Question 02 / 15
How was your current pricing set, and when did you last review it against your actual costs?
Pricing is reviewed at least annually. It's based on cost structure, market positioning, and the value we deliver.
4 pts
We've raised prices once or twice but it wasn't a structured review. More reactive than strategic.
2 pts
Pricing was set a while ago. I know I should revisit it but it hasn't happened.
1 pt
Pricing was based on what felt right at the time or what competitors were charging. I've never properly costed it.

Question 03 / 15
How concentrated is your revenue? What would happen if your top two clients or revenue streams disappeared tomorrow?
We're well-diversified. No single client or stream represents more than 20% of revenue.
4 pts
We have some concentration but it's manageable. One or two clients matter a lot but we have others.
2 pts
One or two clients represent more than 40% of revenue. Losing them would be serious.
1 pt
The majority of our revenue comes from one or two sources. It keeps me up at night.
0 pts
Question 04 / 15
Is profit in your business a deliberate outcome, or something that's left over after everything else?
Deliberate. We set a profit target, build our cost structure around it, and track it against that target.
4 pts
Partly deliberate. We have a sense of what we want to make but the structure isn't built around it explicitly.
2 pts
Mostly leftover. Profit is what remains after we've paid for everything. Some months there's more, some months less.
1 pt
Profit is a hope, not a plan. I'm not sure our current model is actually profitable in a structural sense.

Question 05 / 15
Does your business have a cash reserve that would let it survive 60+ days without new revenue?
Yes. We have a deliberate cash buffer and I know exactly how long we could run on it.
4 pts

2 pts

1 pt
No. Cash is tight. A bad month would be a real problem.

Pillar 02
Team & Cost Structure
These questions examine whether the cost of your people and operations is proportional to the output and growth they enable, or whether it's gradually suffocating the business.
Question 06 / 15
Do you know your total cost per head, and does the output of each role justify that cost?
Yes. I know what each role costs fully loaded (salary, tax, benefits, overhead) and I can connect it to revenue or output.
4 pts
I know salaries but I haven't calculated the full cost per head or tried to connect it to output.
2 pts
Not really. The team has grown and costs have grown with it but I've never broken it down that way.
1 pt

Pillar 03
Systems & Founder Dependency
These questions get at the part founders most often avoid: how much of the business runs because of you, and what would actually happen if you weren't there. This is where most businesses quietly break.
Question 11 / 15
If you disappeared for two weeks with no access to email or phone, what would actually stop?
Almost nothing. The team handles operations well. I'd come back to a business that had continued running.
4 pts
Some things would slow down but not stop. There'd be issues but nothing catastrophic.
2 pts
Several important things would stop or go badly. Key decisions and client relationships need me.
1 pt
A lot would stop. I'm the bottleneck for most things that actually matter. I'd never actually do this.
0 pts
Question 12 / 15
Are your core business processes documented? Could someone new follow them without asking you?
Yes. We have documented processes for the key parts of the business. They're used and maintained.
4 pts
Some are. We've documented the obvious things but a lot still lives in people's heads.
2 pts
Mostly not. Documentation is on the list but it never makes it to the top.
1 pt
No. The business runs on tribal knowledge and I'm the main source of it.
0 pts
Question 13 / 15

Very few. We have clear authority levels. People make decisions within their lanes without coming to me.
4 pts
Some. There are a few areas where I'm still the default but I'm working on changing that.
2 pts
Many. Most decisions above a certain size come to me. I'm often the last stop before anything moves.
1 pt
Almost everything of any importance. I'm the decision-maker for the whole business and it's exhausting.
0 pts
Question 14 / 15
Do you have a regular cadence for reviewing your business performance, or is it only when something goes wrong?
Regular cadence. We review key metrics weekly or monthly in a structured way. It drives decisions.
4 pts
Monthly or so. It happens but it's not always structured or consistently useful.
2 pts
Quarterly at best. I review numbers when something prompts it, not as a rhythm.
1 pt
Mostly reactive. I look at the numbers when something feels wrong, not as a regular practice.
0 pts

Possible and planned. We're actively building toward that. There's a clear structure that could run without me.
4 pts
Possible in theory. I'd like it to be, and we're making progress, but there's still a lot of me in the business.
2 pts
Unlikely in that timeframe. The business isn't structured to run without me yet and I'm not sure how to get there.
1 pt
Impossible right now. The business and I are the same thing. That's the problem I'm trying to solve.

Key findings
Strength
Profit intent is there
You're thinking about profit more deliberately than most founders at your stage. Pricing has been reviewed, you have some sense of margin by area, and you have a cash buffer. These aren't small things. Many founders I work with don't have any of them.
Worth watching
Cost structure is silently growing
The team has grown and some roles have become undefined over time. This is very common and rarely deliberate. It usually happens when good people get hired for one purpose and quietly absorb tasks that belong somewhere else. The cost stays. The clarity goes. It's worth stopping to look at what each role actually costs fully loaded and what would stop if that person left.
Priority
The business lives in your head and it's costing you
Your systems score points to the most common and most expensive pattern I see in founder-led businesses: the founder is the system. Decisions wait for you. Processes aren't written down. Two weeks away would be genuinely hard. This isn't a personality flaw. It's a structural gap. And it doesn't fix itself by working harder. It fixes by building differently.
Based on your results, here's what to do next
01
Start with the systems pillar, not the numbers. Until decisions can be made without you, the other two pillars will keep underperforming.
02
Map what you do in a week. Not your job title, what you actually do. Then ask which of those things should never be done by you again.
03
Do a full cost per head calculation. Include tax, benefits, tools, and overhead. Compare it to what you know about output.
